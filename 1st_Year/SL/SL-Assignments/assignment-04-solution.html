The survival time to poison dataset

The surtime.txt file contains the survival time to poison data set. The data give the survival times (in 10 hour units) in a 3 x 4 factorial experiment, the factors being (a) three poisons and (b) four treatments. Each combination of the two factors is used for four animals, the allocation to animals being completely randomized.


1. Load the surtime data and make sure that the variables Poison and Treatment are properly defined to have class factor.
surtime <- read.table("surtime.txt", head=TRUE)
is.factor(surtime$Poison)
## [1] FALSE
is.factor(surtime$Treatment)
## [1] FALSE
surtime$Poison    <- as.factor(surtime$Poison)
surtime$Treatment <- as.factor(surtime$Treatment)


2. Apply one-way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) to compare the mean survival times of the tree poisons. Include a Bartlett test to check the assumption of equal variances and a post-hoc analysis based on Tukey HSD (honestly significant difference) method. Comment the results you obtain.
boxplot(surtime$Time~ surtime$Poison)

# test for equal variances 
bartlett.test(Time~ Poison, data=surtime)
## 
##  Bartlett test of homogeneity of variances
## 
## data:  Time by Poison
## Bartlett's K-squared = 25.88, df = 2, p-value = 2.4e-06
# ANOVA table
aov.poison <- aov(Time~ Poison, data=surtime)
summary(aov.poison)
##             Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value   Pr(>F)    
## Poison       2  1.033  0.5165   11.79 7.66e-05 ***
## Residuals   45  1.972  0.0438                     
## ---
## Signif. codes:  0 '***' 0.001 '**' 0.01 '*' 0.05 '.' 0.1 ' ' 1
# Tukey post hoc analysis
tukey.poison <- TukeyHSD(aov.poison)
tukey.poison
##   Tukey multiple comparisons of means
##     95% family-wise confidence level
## 
## Fit: aov(formula = Time ~ Poison, data = surtime)
## 
## $Poison
##          diff        lwr         upr     p adj
## 2-1 -0.073125 -0.2525046  0.10625464 0.5881654
## 3-1 -0.341250 -0.5206296 -0.16187036 0.0000971
## 3-2 -0.268125 -0.4475046 -0.08874536 0.0020924
par(mfrow=c(1, 2))
plot(Time~ Poison, data=surtime)
plot(tukey.poison)

par(mfrow=c(1,1))

Conclusions:

  1. According to the Bartlett test we cannot accept the assumption of equal variances, and thus the results of the ANOVA analysis are not fully reliable.

  2. The ANOVA table shows that the hypothesis that the mean survival times of the three poisons are not equal is highly significant, and should thus be accepted.

  3. The Tukey post hoc analysis gives insight into the three pairwise mean comparisons, thereby showing that the difference among poisons is mainly due to poison 3 whose mean survival time seems to be significantly shorter than both that of poison 1 and poison 2.

We can see that the interaction effect between the two variables is represented by 6 interaction parameters and the interaction coefficient corresponding to the Poison3:Treatment2 seems to be significantly different to zero. However, this could be due to a first type error: we are considering 6 test procedures and the overall significance level of this procedure is not known and, possibly, much greater than the desired 0.05 significance level. Hence, we carry out a single F-test for the null hypothesis that all the 6 interaction coefficients are equal to zero.

lmfit.null <- lm(Time~ Poison+Treatment, data=surtime)
anova(lmfit.null, lmfit)
## Analysis of Variance Table
## 
## Model 1: Time ~ Poison + Treatment
## Model 2: Time ~ Poison * Treatment

## 1     42 1.05086                           
## 2     36 0.80073  6   0.25014 1.8743 0.1123

The latter shows that, at the 0.05 significance level, there is not empirical evidence that Poison and Treatment significantly interact.
